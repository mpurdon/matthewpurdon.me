# Writing Without the AI Fingerprints

A practical style guide for preserving a clear, human, opinionated voice when using AI as a writing assistant.

---

## Purpose

AI can make prose smoother, cleaner, and more complete. It can also make every paragraph feel engineered, every transition feel announced, and every conclusion feel like it was designed for a quote card.

The goal is not to hide AI use. The goal is to prevent AI assistance from flattening your judgment, cadence, and personality into generic high-polish editorial prose.

Use AI to challenge, research, organize, and edit. Do not let it decide what every paragraph should sound like.

---

## Core Principle

**Write the argument yourself. Use AI to improve the draft, not to invent the voice.**

Before using AI, write down:

- What you actually believe
- What you want the reader to reconsider
- Which examples come from your own experience
- What trade-off or tension matters most
- What you are still uncertain about

If those elements do not exist before drafting, the model will often substitute structure, symmetry, and rhetorical confidence for actual thinking.

---

## 1. Stop Over-Architecting the Essay

AI tends to produce essays that feel mechanically complete:

1. Introduce the problem
2. Reframe the problem
3. Present three supporting examples
4. Reconcile the examples
5. State the larger lesson
6. End with a polished inversion

This structure is useful, but repetition makes the article feel generated.

### Prefer

- Uneven section lengths
- Natural detours
- One example explored deeply instead of three examples treated symmetrically
- Sections that end with facts, questions, or unresolved tension
- Transitions that are implied rather than announced

### Avoid

- “Now the two halves of this argument meet.”
- “There is one more thing this gives us.”
- “This brings us to the larger point.”
- “The lesson is clear.”
- “Taken together, these examples reveal…”

### Editing rule

Delete at least half of the sentences that explain how the essay is structured. If the structure works, the reader should feel it without being told.

---

## 2. Do Not End Every Section With a Mic-Drop Line

AI is very good at compressing paragraphs into aphorisms:

- “Scarcity rationed carelessness.”
- “The unit of reuse is the job, not the artifact.”
- “Simple means somebody decided.”

A few of these are memorable. Too many make the prose feel relentlessly manufactured.

### Prefer

Use one genuinely strong line where it matters most. Let other sections end plainly.

A section can end with:

- A specific fact
- A consequence
- A question
- A concession
- An unfinished tension
- A practical next step

### Avoid

Ending every section with:

- A reversal
- A slogan
- A fragment
- A sentence beginning with “The real…”
- A sentence shaped like “X is not Y. X is Z.”
- A two-line dramatic conclusion

### Editing rule

Highlight every sentence that could appear alone on LinkedIn. Keep the best 20 percent.

---

## 3. Reduce Engineered Reversals

AI-generated prose often relies on contrast templates:

- “It is not X. It is Y.”
- “The difference is not A. It is B.”
- “That is true, and beside the point.”
- “This is not a compromise. It is the thesis.”
- “The tool is not the product. The outcome is.”

These are effective in moderation. Repeated use becomes a fingerprint.

### Prefer

State the distinction directly.

Instead of:

> The bottleneck is not compute. The bottleneck is judgment.

Write:

> Compute matters, but judgment is usually where the project succeeds or fails.

Instead of:

> This is not a model registry. It is a capability registry.

Write:

> The registry should catalogue deployable capabilities rather than model files.

### Editing rule

Search for:

- `not`
- `isn't`
- `rather`
- `instead`
- `the real`
- `the difference`

Rewrite at least half of the contrast constructions as direct statements.

---

## 4. Let Some Paragraphs Simply Explain Things

AI often treats every paragraph as a performance unit. Each one introduces tension, develops it, and resolves it into a lesson.

Human writing is usually less uniform. Some paragraphs exist only to clarify a mechanism, add context, or describe what happened.

### Prefer

Include paragraphs that:

- Explain without persuading
- Describe without interpreting
- Introduce evidence without immediately converting it into a thesis
- Admit that a detail is interesting but not decisive

### Avoid

Forcing every anecdote to prove the central argument.

### Editing rule

For every three argumentative paragraphs, allow one paragraph to be informational, descriptive, or exploratory.

---

## 5. Use Fewer Perfectly Balanced Lists

AI likes completeness and parallelism:

- Cost, latency, privacy, security, governance, sovereignty, accessibility
- First, second, third
- Research, synthesis, implementation, evaluation
- Three examples with matching lengths and structures

Balanced lists can make an essay sound comprehensive while hiding which elements actually matter.

### Prefer

- Rank items
- Emphasize one or two
- Admit that the list is incomplete
- Use prose when the categories are not genuinely parallel
- Remove items included only for completeness

### Avoid

Lists where every item has the same grammatical structure but not the same importance.

### Editing rule

For every long list, ask:

1. Which two items actually carry the argument?
2. Which item was added only to make the list feel complete?
3. Would the paragraph improve if the list were uneven?

---

## 6. Preserve Specificity, Including Awkward Specificity

AI tends to generalize personal experiences into clean lessons. That removes the details that make writing credible.

### Prefer

- Exact tools
- Exact constraints
- Specific mistakes
- Unflattering details
- Odd edge cases
- What you originally misunderstood
- What changed your mind

Instead of:

> We learned that observability must be designed in from the start.

Write:

> We had logs, but they were spread across three systems and none of them carried the transfer ID. We could see that something failed without seeing which customer was affected.

Specificity is more persuasive than polish.

### Editing rule

Add at least one detail that a generic writer could not have invented.

---

## 7. Keep Some Uncertainty

AI often resolves ambiguity too aggressively. It prefers conclusions that sound finished.

Human expertise often sounds like:

- “I am not sure this generalizes.”
- “This worked in our case, but the constraint mattered.”
- “The obvious answer may still be right.”
- “I have changed my mind on this.”
- “The evidence is weaker here.”
- “There are cases where I would do the opposite.”

### Prefer

Qualified confidence.

### Avoid

- Universal lessons from one example
- Certainty unsupported by evidence
- Conclusions stronger than the article's research
- Treating an architectural preference as a law

### Editing rule

Identify the three strongest claims. Add scope, conditions, or counterexamples where appropriate.

---

## 8. Vary Cadence Naturally

AI-assisted prose often falls into a recognizable rhythm:

> Medium sentence. Medium sentence. Short fragment.  
> Longer explanatory sentence.  
> Punch line.

Repeated fragments and abrupt one-line paragraphs create artificial dramatic emphasis.

### Prefer

- Paragraphs of different lengths
- Occasional long sentences when the idea requires them
- Fewer standalone fragments
- Natural transitions inside paragraphs
- A mix of declarative, interrogative, and qualified sentences

### Avoid

- Repeated one-sentence paragraphs
- Repeated sentence fragments
- Every section ending in a short line
- Excessive em dashes
- Identical paragraph shapes

### Editing rule

Read the piece aloud. Mark any rhythm that repeats three times. Rewrite one occurrence.

---

## 9. Stop Announcing Importance

AI frequently tells the reader what matters before demonstrating it.

### Avoid

- “Crucially”
- “Importantly”
- “It is worth noting”
- “The key insight”
- “The deeper point”
- “What matters here”
- “This distinction is critical”
- “The implications are profound”

### Prefer

Put the important fact in the sentence and let it carry its own weight.

Instead of:

> Crucially, the evaluation runs on platform-owned compute.

Write:

> The evaluation runs on platform-owned compute, so vendors cannot select favourable hardware or omit failed runs.

### Editing rule

Delete the importance marker. If the sentence becomes weak, strengthen the evidence rather than restoring the marker.

---

## 10. Avoid Synthetic Comprehensiveness

AI can produce a suspiciously complete paragraph that covers every dimension of a problem in one sweep.

This often appears as:

> The system must account for privacy, sovereignty, bilingual access, procurement, tenancy, security, auditability, Indigenous data governance, cost, performance, and regional availability.

The paragraph may be correct, but it sounds assembled rather than lived.

### Prefer

Group concerns by decision.

For example:

> The registry needs enough governance metadata to answer three questions: who may use the capability, where its data may travel, and how its performance was verified.

Then introduce additional requirements only when they become relevant.

### Editing rule

Replace encyclopedic coverage with decision-oriented coverage.

---

## 11. Use Sources as Evidence, Not Scenery

AI-assisted articles sometimes accumulate references to create authority. Each source appears briefly, gets summarized neatly, and is folded into the thesis.

### Prefer

- Engage deeply with fewer sources
- Explain why a source changed or challenged your view
- Distinguish primary evidence from commentary
- Note disagreement between sources
- Leave some source material uninterpreted

### Avoid

- One paragraph per source
- Three-source bundles designed to look comprehensive
- Citations that support atmosphere rather than claims
- Treating all sources as equally reliable

### Editing rule

For each source, write one sentence answering:

> What would be weaker, less precise, or less credible without this source?

Remove sources with no good answer.

---

## 12. Do Not Let AI Over-Normalize Your Voice

Editing models often remove the very things that make a writer recognizable:

- Informal asides
- Mild irritation
- Dry humour
- Domain-specific shorthand
- Slightly unusual phrasing
- Uneven emphasis
- Strong preferences
- Sentences that are clear but not “elegant”

### Prefer

Keep phrases you would naturally say aloud.

It is acceptable to write:

- “That seems backwards to me.”
- “This is where things get weird.”
- “I do not buy that argument.”
- “The boring answer is probably the right one.”
- “You can do this, but I would not.”

### Avoid

Allowing AI to replace these with:

- “This reveals a deeper tension.”
- “A more nuanced interpretation is required.”
- “The conventional framing is insufficient.”
- “This underscores the importance of…”

### Editing rule

After AI editing, restore at least three phrases that sound unmistakably like you.

## 13. Separate the Argument From the Drafting

When the same model invents the argument and writes the prose in one pass, the result is generic AI architecture. The protection is not who types the first draft; it is that the argument exists, in Matthew's words, before drafting starts.

### How this works on this site

The argument develops in conversation before anything gets drafted: idea fragments, pasted threads, scoping decisions, reactions, disagreements. That conversation is the thesis note, and it usually contains far more of Matthew's actual positions than a 200-word note would. AI then writes the first full draft in Matthew's voice.

The cost of that trade, named plainly: whoever writes the first sentences sets the cadence baseline, and the revision rounds get spent sanding the drafter's fingerprints off. Budget for it (a long piece can take dozens of rounds), and convert every caught fingerprint into a standing rule so it is paid for once.

1. The argument accumulates in conversation. Nothing drafts until the spine, the stance, and the title direction are Matthew's decisions.
2. AI verifies every anecdote, number, and quote against real sources before it enters the draft. No invented biography, ever.
3. AI writes the first draft in the two house registers (tight prose, loose soapboxes), against the voice guides and the banned-phrases list.
4. Before showing the draft, AI runs the fingerprint checklist and the Final Humanization Pass on its own output. Matthew's rounds should be spent on taste and argument, not on catching "load-bearing" for the third time.
5. Matthew's revision rounds: line edits, structural calls, additions. His hand edits to the file are canon; never revert them.
6. Every new tell caught in the rounds becomes a banned-phrase rule immediately, in the memory guides and (if general) in this guide's checklist.
7. Before shipping: a dedup pass (each claim lives in exactly one place), frontmatter/read-time check, then log the rounds in `writing-log.md`.

### Alternative mode

For pieces where the voice matters most, invert it: Matthew writes the rough sentences first and AI acts purely as the skeptical editor described elsewhere in this guide. Correcting your own cadence is cheaper than correcting someone else's.

### Avoid

> Research this topic and write a polished 1,500-word article with a strong narrative and memorable conclusion.

That prompt almost guarantees generic AI architecture, because it asks the model to invent the argument, not just the sentences.

---

## 14. Give AI Narrow Editing Jobs

Broad requests encourage the model to rewrite your voice.

### Good prompts

- “Identify claims that need evidence. Do not rewrite.”
- “Mark repeated ideas.”
- “Find transitions that over-explain the structure.”
- “List sentences that sound like slogans.”
- “Point out where the conclusion is stronger than the evidence.”
- “Find sections where the cadence repeats.”
- “Suggest cuts only.”
- “Flag generic phrases without replacing them.”
- “Tell me where the argument becomes too neat.”

### Risky prompts

- “Make this more compelling.”
- “Make this flow better.”
- “Make it sound professional.”
- “Improve the writing.”
- “Add punch.”
- “Make every section stronger.”
- “Rewrite this in a thought-leadership style.”

---

## 15. Edit Against the Model's Default Taste

AI tends to prefer:

- Symmetry
- Completeness
- Explicit transitions
- Strong topic sentences
- Memorable closers
- Clean binaries
- Confidence
- Abstract nouns
- Formal polish

A human editor should deliberately reintroduce:

- Unevenness
- Omission
- Friction
- Specificity
- Digression
- Doubt
- Abruptness
- Personal judgment

The goal is not messiness. The goal is writing that feels selected rather than generated.

## Common AI Fingerprints Checklist

Search the draft for these patterns.

### Structural fingerprints

- Every section has the same length
- Every section ends with a lesson
- The introduction previews the entire essay
- The conclusion restates every major point
- Transitions announce the argument's structure
- Three examples are presented with perfect symmetry
- The essay resolves every tension

### Sentence-level fingerprints

- “It is not X. It is Y.”
- “The real question is…”
- “The deeper point is…”
- “This is where X meets Y.”
- “Taken together…”
- “In other words…”
- “That distinction matters.”
- “This changes everything.”
- “The lesson is clear.”
- “Not because X, but because Y.”
- Frequent em dashes
- Frequent standalone fragments
- Repeated rhetorical questions
- Repeated three-item lists

### Tone fingerprints

- Relentless confidence
- Constant polish
- No failed ideas
- No uncertainty
- No irrelevant but memorable details
- No signs that the writer changed their mind
- Every anecdote supports the thesis perfectly
- Every paragraph sounds publishable in isolation

## Final Humanization Pass

Before publishing, perform these edits.

### Cut

- Half of the structural transitions
- Most “importance” markers
- Repeated thesis statements
- Weak aphorisms
- Lists added for completeness
- Conclusions that merely restate the introduction
- Paragraphs whose only purpose is to sound definitive

### Add

- One concrete failure
- One personal constraint
- One unresolved question
- One counterexample
- One place where your confidence is limited
- One detail that only you would know
- One sentence you would actually say in conversation

### Check

- Does every paragraph need to resolve?
- Are too many sentences quotable?
- Does the article sound more certain than I am?
- Did AI make the structure too visible?
- Did the model remove my irritation, humour, or skepticism?
- Are the examples carrying the argument, or merely decorating it?
- Is the conclusion earned?
- Could a competent AI have written this without my experience?

## Reference Workflow (writer-drafts-first mode)

The default on this site is the conversation-first workflow in section 13. The version below is the stricter mode for when Matthew drafts the sentences himself.

### 1. Thesis note

Write 100–200 words without AI:

- What happened?
- What do I think it means?
- Why do I care?
- What would a reasonable person disagree with?

### 2. Evidence gathering

Use AI to locate:

- Primary sources
- Counterarguments
- Contradictory examples
- Missing context
- Claims requiring verification

Do not ask it to draft.

### 3. Rough draft

Write quickly in your own voice. Leave gaps and placeholders.

### 4. Adversarial review

Ask AI:

> Identify where this argument is weak, too neat, repetitive, unsupported, or overconfident. Do not rewrite the article.

### 5. Manual revision

Resolve the criticism yourself.

### 6. Fingerprint review

Ask AI:

> Mark sentences and structural patterns that resemble generic AI-assisted editorial prose. Focus on reversals, aphorisms, explicit transitions, symmetry, and over-complete lists. Do not suggest replacement prose.

### 7. Mechanical cleanup

Use AI only for:

- Grammar
- Typos
- Broken references
- Repeated words
- Inconsistent terminology

Require it to preserve sentence structure unless a sentence is genuinely unclear.

## A Useful Default Prompt

```text
Act as a skeptical editor, not a ghostwriter.

Do not rewrite my prose.

Identify:
1. Claims that need evidence
2. Repeated ideas
3. Over-explained transitions
4. Engineered reversals
5. Aphorisms or mic-drop lines that feel excessive
6. Sections that are too symmetrical
7. Lists that feel artificially comprehensive
8. Places where the conclusion is stronger than the evidence
9. Sentences that do not sound like natural spoken language
10. Details that feel generic rather than personally observed

Preserve my voice, informality, and unevenness. Prefer cuts and questions over replacement prose.
```

## House Rule

**AI may help make the writing clearer. It may not make every paragraph impressive.**

The reader should remember the idea, the evidence, and the writer's judgment—not the smoothness of the prose.
